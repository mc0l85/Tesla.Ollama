- CRISPR-Cas9, originally a bacterial immune system, was repurposed by Doudna and Charpentier for precise DNA editing, earning them the 2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.  
- The technology is now widely applied across agriculture (disease-resistant crops), medicine (e.g., FDA-approved sickle cell therapy in 2023), and basic research.  
- Despite ethical controversies like germline editing (exemplified by the 2018 He Jiankui case), CRISPR faces strong commercial momentum with over $5 billion invested in startups in 2023.
