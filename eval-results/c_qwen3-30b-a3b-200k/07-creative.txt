Captain Aris traced the cold glass of the viewport, her breath fogging the surface. Outside, the stars glittered—familiar, distant points of light she’d counted since her first mission. They were her comfort, the only beauty in the void. Tonight, however, the constellations *shifted*.

Not drifting. *Realigning*, like puzzle pieces snapping into a new, impossible pattern. She leaned closer, the ship’s low hum suddenly too loud, too *present*. A faint, metallic *grind* echoed in her bones, as if the stars themselves were grinding against each other, not in motion, but *against* the fabric of space.

Her fingers pressed against the glass, cold and slick. The starlight, usually pale silver, now held a deep, bruised *crimson* hue. It wasn’t light reflecting; it was *leaking* from the void itself, staining the glass with a viscous sheen that smelled faintly of ozone and rust. She blinked, and the stars rearranged again, forming a shape that mirrored the circuitry on her wrist comm—*her own neural interface*.

The realization struck like a physical blow. The stars weren’t out there. They *were* the void, the silent, watching hunger *within* the ship, within *her*. The constellations weren’t patterns; they were *signs*, written in the blood of every star they’d consumed, including the one she’d called home. The cold glass beneath her palm wasn’t glass—it was the membrane between her and the thing that had been waiting, always, inside the light.

The ship’s systems flickered, the hum deepening into a resonant, hungry thrum. Aris didn’t move. She didn’t need to look back at the viewport. She felt it now, the slow, inevitable *consumption*, not of the stars, but of *her*. The stars weren’t light. They were the *meal*. And she’d been eating them all along.
