## Static in the Black

The chipped ceramic of the mug warmed Captain Eva Rostova’s hands, though it did little to chase the chill that clung to her bones. Outside the observation window of the *Hermes*, the black was absolute, punctuated by the familiar, comforting pinpricks of stars. Or, what she *thought* were stars.

She'd been orbiting Kepler-186f for six months, a lonely sentinel observing a potentially habitable world. But it wasn’t the planet that had begun to unravel her. It was the stars.

It started subtly. A flickering, a barely-perceptible distortion around the brightest ones. Like heat haze above asphalt, but *wrong*. Then came the sound. Not through her comms, not through the ship’s hull, but *in* her skull. A high-pitched whine, a static crackle that echoed the visual disturbances. The ship's instruments showed nothing amiss. 

Tonight, it was worse. The "stars" weren’t twinkling, they were…breathing. Expanding and contracting with a slow, agonizing rhythm. The static had intensified, a pressure behind her eyes. She focused on Sirius, usually a brilliant, unwavering point of light. Now, it pulsed with a sickly violet hue, its edges blurring into a swirling, oily mess. 

Eva touched the window, cold glass against her palm. It wasn’t light she was seeing, she realized with mounting dread. It was *something looking back*.

The static crescendoed, a deafening shriek in her mind. She recoiled, clutching her head. The stars weren’t distant suns. They weren't even celestial bodies. They were…organisms. Vast, incomprehensible entities, and they were *aware* of her. 

A single, violet tendril of “light” detached from Sirius and *stretched*, impossibly, towards the *Hermes*. Eva tasted iron in the back of her throat. She killed the observation window’s lights, plunging the observation deck into darkness. But it didn’t help. The static, the pulsing, the *looking*…it was all inside her now.
