The hum of the *Daedalus* was the only constant in Elias’s life now. A low, vibrating thrum that resonated in his teeth. Six months into a solo mission to chart the Xylos sector, and the endless black had begun to feel less like space, and more like a suffocating void pressing in on all sides. He’d always found a strange comfort in the distant, diamond-dust stars, a familiar beauty. Until tonight.

He was calibrating the long-range scanner, the metallic tang of coolant sharp in the recycled air, when he noticed it. A flicker. Not a twinkle, not the usual distortion of light through atmospheric interference – this was *wrong*. He zoomed in on the constellation Lyra. The stars weren’t points of light. They were… textures. Like scales.

He adjusted the scanner’s frequency, pushing it beyond its designed limits. The image sharpened, and nausea rose in his throat. The “stars” weren't burning gas, but colossal, iridescent surfaces. Each one pulsed with a slow, rhythmic undulation, like breathing. They weren't distant; they were *close*. Impossibly, terrifyingly close. 

He ran diagnostics. Everything checked out. The scanner wasn’t malfunctioning. He tried other constellations – Orion, Cassiopeia, the comforting Plough – all the same. Shifting, breathing, *organic*. A chilling realization dawned: the universe wasn't an empty canvas dotted with fire, but the skin of something vast and ancient. 

Elias cut comms. He didn’t want to report this. Didn’t want anyone to know. The hum of the *Daedalus* now felt like a frantic heartbeat against the slow, deliberate rhythm of the universe around him. He switched off the scanner, plunging the bridge back into near darkness. But the image remained, burned onto the backs of his eyelids – a galaxy of scales, silently watching, patiently waiting. He was adrift, not amongst stars, but *within* something unknowable and deeply, deeply wrong.
