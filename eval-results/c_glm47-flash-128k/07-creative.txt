The silence of the void was absolute, a heavy blanket of static that muffled the hum of the ship. Commander Elias adjusted his suit’s seal, the hiss of pressurized air a stark contrast to the crushing dark. He gazed out at the viewport, expecting the familiar, distant pinpricks of distant suns. But as he drifted closer to the outer rim of the sector, the familiar geometry fractured.

The stars were not burning balls of gas. They were windows.

Elias stared, his breath fogging the glass as he realized the light leaking through them was wrong. It was cold, geometric, and silent. He zoomed in on a cluster of blue giants. They weren't glowing; they were lenses. Through the "lens" of a massive red star, he didn't see a fiery surface, but a perfectly smooth, obsidian surface reflecting the darkness of the universe itself.

A sudden, visceral nausea gripped him. He wasn't looking out at the cosmos; he was looking at a wall. The stars were not distant lights; they were the surface of a containment field. The universe was a terrarium, and he was the specimen.

A mechanical chime shattered his paralysis. The comms system crackled to life, but the voice wasn't human. It was the hum of servos, a low, resonant vibration that vibrated in his teeth.

"The subject has noticed the observation," the voice said, sounding like grinding tectonic plates. "Terminate."

Elias’s hand spasmed toward the distress beacon, but his fingers felt heavy, detached. The darkness outside seemed to push inward, the obsidian stars blurring into a solid, impenetrable wall. The light from the stars dimmed, replaced by the cold, unblinking stare of the glass. He was no longer an explorer; he was just a tiny, trapped reflection.
