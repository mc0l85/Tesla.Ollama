**The Flicker**  

Commander Elara Voss floated in the silence of the *Odyssey*, her breath fogging the visor of her helmet. The stars outside her cockpit pulsed like dying embers—flickering, uneven, as if the universe itself were gasping. She’d seen them for years, these distant suns, but tonight, their light felt *wrong*.  

Her gloved hand brushed the control panel, its metal cold as a corpse’s skin. The ship’s hum, usually a steady thrum, stuttered. A warning light blinked crimson. *Oxygen levels dropping.* She frowned. The air tasted metallic, sharp, like blood on her tongue.  

Then she saw it: a star, once steady, now *twitching*. Not a supernova—no, that would be too loud. This was subtler. A flicker, like a candle in a draft. But the draft wasn’t there. The stars didn’t flicker.  

Elara’s pulse thrummed in her ears. She leaned closer to the viewport, her breath fogging the glass. The stars weren’t just moving—they were *hiding*. Their light bent, warped, as if the void itself were a lens. A shadow passed over one, and for a heartbeat, she saw *something* behind it: not a planet, not a nebula, but a *presence*, vast and unblinking.  

The ship shuddered. A warning klaxon blared. Elara’s hands trembled. The oxygen gauge dropped, each second a needle sliding toward red. She tried to radio Mission Control, but static hissed back, distorted, as if the stars were speaking through a broken mouth.  

Then the lights died.  

Darkness. Not the soft black of space, but a *hungry* black, swallowing the stars whole. Her helmet’s glow flickered, then died. In the silence, she heard it—a low, resonant hum, not from the ship, but from *inside* her skull. The stars weren’t distant. They were *close*. And they were *watching*.  

When the rescue team found her three days later, her logbook was filled with frantic scrawls: *“They’re not stars. They’re eyes. And they’re waking up.”* The last entry ended with a single, shuddering line: *“I can’t unsee them.”*  

The stars still flicker. Somewhere, they’re waiting.
