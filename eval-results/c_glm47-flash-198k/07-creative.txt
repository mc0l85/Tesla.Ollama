The silence of the void was usually a blanket of peace, but today, it felt like a heavy, wet wool pressing against my suit’s helmet. I floated near the observation deck, sipping recycled coffee that tasted of tin and regret, staring at the Milky Way.

I had spent years looking at the cosmos as a gallery of distant, burning suns. But tonight, something shifted. The constellations seemed to pulse, a slow, rhythmic heartbeat that synchronized with my own. I reached out, my gloved hand trembling, to touch the Andromeda galaxy.

It wasn’t a cloud of gas and dust. It was a lattice of bone.

I pulled back in horror, my breath hitching in my throat. The "stars" weren't distant points of light; they were the microscopic, glowing dust of millions of long-dead organisms, suspended in a suspension of dark, viscous fluid. I looked closer at the nearby constellations. The "Big Dipper" was a cluster of tiny, skeletal ribs. The "North Star" was nothing but a single, unblinking, crystalline eye.

The realization hit me with the force of a physical blow. We weren't floating in an empty void; we were swimming inside the carcass of a god. The universe wasn't vast and empty; it was dead, preserved in amber.

A low, thrumming vibration began to emanate from the walls of the station—no, from the universe itself. The fluid around me began to churn, the temperature rising until my skin burned. The "stars" weren't watching me; they were waking up. I wasn't an explorer of the stars; I was just food.
