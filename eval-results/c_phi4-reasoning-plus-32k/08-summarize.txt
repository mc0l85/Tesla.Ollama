• CRISPR-Cas9 was discovered as a bacterial immune mechanism and reprogrammed in 2012 by Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier to cut DNA at precise locations—a breakthrough that earned them the 2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.

• The technology has been widely applied across fields, including agriculture (developing disease-resistant crops), medicine (FDA-approved CRISPR therapy for sickle cell disease), and basic research (systematically studying gene function).

• Despite its potential, ethical concerns—especially regarding germline editing, highlighted by He Jiankui’s 2018 gene-edited babies—persist, yet commercial investment in CRISPR startups exceeded $5 billion in 2023, showing strong confidence in its therapeutic promise.
