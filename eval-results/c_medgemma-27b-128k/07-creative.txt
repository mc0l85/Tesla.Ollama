The hum of the life support system was the only constant in Commander Eva Rostova's solitary existence aboard the Kepler-186f observation module. For six months, she'd stared out the reinforced viewport, charting the constellations, mapping nebulae, a lonely sentinel in the inky black. The stars were her companions, pinpricks of ancient light, comforting in their predictable, distant grandeur.

Then came the anomaly. A flicker, a distortion, like heat rising off asphalt, but in the cold vacuum. It started subtly, a shimmering around Proxima Centauri, the closest star. Eva initially dismissed it as equipment malfunction, a trick of the aging optics. But it persisted, grew bolder, spreading like a stain across the celestial canvas.

One night, the distortion enveloped Sirius. The familiar, brilliant blue-white point fractured, splintered into a thousand iridescent shards. A low, guttural hum, not mechanical, resonated through the module, vibrating in Eva's teeth. She pressed her face against the viewport, her breath fogging the cold glass. The shards coalesced, reformed, but not as Sirius. It was…wrong. A pulsating, fleshy mass, veined with sickly green light, hung where the star had been.

Panic clawed at her throat. She checked the sensors, the external cameras. Nothing. The instruments registered the star as normal, but her eyes screamed otherwise. The hum intensified, morphing into a chorus of whispers, too low to understand, yet chillingly intimate.

Eva stumbled back, the metallic tang of fear coating her tongue. She looked at the other stars. They were changing too, subtly at first, then with horrifying speed. The comforting pinpricks were becoming grotesque, organic shapes, pulsating with an alien life she couldn't comprehend. The universe wasn't a vast, empty void dotted with distant suns. It was a festering wound, and she was the only one who could see it. The hum became a roar, and the stars began to sing.
