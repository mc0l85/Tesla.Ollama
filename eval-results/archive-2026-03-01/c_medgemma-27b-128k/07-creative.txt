The hum of the life support system was the only constant in Commander Eva Rostova's solitary existence aboard the Kepler-186f orbital station. For six months, she'd stared out the viewport, charting the constellations, a familiar comfort in the alien black. Tonight, however, the familiar felt wrong.

It started subtly. A flicker in the periphery of the Ursa Major, a distortion like heat haze rising off asphalt. Eva dismissed it as fatigue, the endless void playing tricks on her eyes. But it persisted, growing stronger, spreading like a stain across the velvet canvas. The stars, once pinpricks of unwavering light, began to *breathe*.

They pulsed, not with the steady rhythm of celestial bodies, but with a sickening, organic throb. Colors shifted within their cores – emerald green, bruised purple, a nauseating, fleshy pink. The air in the station grew thick, heavy with a metallic tang that stung her nostrils. The hum of the life support stuttered, choked.

Eva pressed her face against the cold glass, her breath fogging the viewport. The constellations, once comforting guides, now writhed, coalescing into grotesque, unidentifiable shapes. She could almost *hear* them, a low, guttural chorus that vibrated through the station's hull, resonating deep within her bones.

Panic clawed at her throat. The stars weren't distant suns. They were something else, something vast and unknowable, and they were *looking back*. The viewport began to crack, spiderwebbing with fissures that mirrored the chaos in the sky. A single, colossal eye, impossibly large and filled with cold, ancient malice, opened in the heart of Orion. Eva screamed, but the sound was swallowed by the growing, suffocating silence as the stars consumed the void, and her.
