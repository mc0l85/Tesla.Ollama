**The Watcher in the Void**

The first thing Commander Mara Voss noticed when she stepped onto the observation deck was the smell—cold metal and ozone, the faint bite of ionized air that clung to the hull like a whisper. Outside, the blackness was a living thing, a velvet curtain that seemed to pulse with a rhythm she could not name. The stars, those distant pinpricks of ancient fire, hung like glass beads in a sea of nothingness.

She had come to the *Erebus* seeking answers. Humanity’s last hope lay in a probe that could slip beyond the heliosphere and chart the dark between galaxies. Yet, as the ship’s instruments hummed and the screens flickered, a strange distortion crept across the display—a subtle warping of light that made the constellations shift, not in predictable arcs, but in slow, deliberate spirals.

Mara leaned forward, eyes narrowing. The stars were no longer static points; they were breathing, expanding and contracting as if they possessed a pulse. A low hum rose from the ship’s core, resonating through her bones, and she felt the chill of the void seep into her marrow. The air tasted metallic, like the after‑taste of blood on a wound.

She ordered the navigation array to isolate a single point of light—a star that should have been a familiar orange dwarf. Instead, the screen resolved into a lattice of shifting patterns, each node a filament of light that seemed to reach out, touching one another in an intricate web. The web pulsed, and with each beat, a faint, discordant whisper rose—an echo of something vast and ancient, speaking in a language of vibration.

Mara’s breath fogged the glass. The realization struck like a cold blade: the stars were not distant suns, but eyes—watchful, patient, and utterly indifferent. They observed the *Erebus* not as a speck of dust but as a note in a symphony of darkness. The unsettling truth settled over her like a shroud: the cosmos was a vast, sentient void, and humanity was but a fleeting flicker in its endless night.
