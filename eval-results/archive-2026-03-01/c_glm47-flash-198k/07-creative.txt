Commander Halloway drifted in the silence, the hum of the ship’s life support systems the only sound. He stared out the viewport, aching for the familiar, glittering diamond dust of home. Instead, he saw a nebula of bruised purple and sickly green. It wasn’t beautiful; it was viscous.

He adjusted the magnification on the external camera, his breath fogging the glass. He zoomed in on a cluster of stars he had been tracking for hours. They were supposed to be distant suns, burning balls of gas. But as the image sharpened, the light didn't flicker or pulse. It rippled. The stars were actually microscopic, iridescent insects, skittering across the darkness of the void, leaving trails of bioluminescent slime.

Halloway felt a cold sweat prick his forehead. He looked closer at the "galaxy" spinning slowly in the distance. It wasn't a spiral of stars. It was a single, massive, pulsating eye, blinking slowly in the dark.

The ship’s comms crackled, cutting through the silence. It wasn't static; it was the sound of thousands of tiny, wet feet skittering against metal.

"Commander," a voice echoed from the wall. It sounded like the ship itself was whispering. "They see you."

Halloway looked back at the viewport. The swarm of insects was no longer moving randomly. They were converging, swimming through the vacuum toward the hull, drawn to the heat of the life support systems. The massive eye had opened wider, its pupil a black void that seemed to swallow the stars themselves.
