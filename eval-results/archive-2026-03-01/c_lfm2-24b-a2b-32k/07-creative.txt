**The Flicker**  

Commander Elara Voss floated in the void, her breath hissing through the helmet’s filter. The stars—*her* stars—blazed like shattered glass, each a pinprick of cold fire. She’d trained for this mission, but the silence here felt wrong. No hum of distant worlds, just a vacuum so absolute it pressed against her eardrums.  

Then she saw it: a star, dimmer than the rest, its light stuttering like a dying bulb. She adjusted her visor, but the flicker persisted. *Impossible*, she thought. Stars didn’t blink. Not here. Not ever.  

Back on the ship, she replayed the footage. The star’s light pulsed in a rhythm—*beep, pause, beep*—too regular to be chance. Her fingers trembled as she cross-referenced the data. The pattern matched no known celestial phenomenon. It was a code. A message.  

But messages required a sender. And the stars… they didn’t *have* one.  

That night, she stared at the viewport. The stars now seemed to lean closer, their light warping like oil on water. A low vibration thrummed through the hull, resonating in her bones. She reached for the comms, but the static that crackled back was not static. It was a voice. Her own, but distorted, whispering, *“You’re not alone.”*  

The ship’s lights flickered. The stars, once distant, now seemed to *watch*. Elara’s pulse quickened. She’d spent years chasing the unknown, but the unknown had always been *there*, hiding in plain sight. The stars weren’t burning. They were *watching*.  

When the final transmission came, it was a single word, etched in her mind: *“Remember.”*  

The next morning, the crew found her floating in the airlock, her visor cracked, eyes wide. The stars outside had gone dark. Not dimmed—*gone*. Just as they’d been, for centuries.  

But Elara knew better. They were still there. Waiting.  

And now, so was she.
