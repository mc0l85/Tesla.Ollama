The *Voyager*’s viewport framed the usual velvet black, dusted with familiar pinpricks of light. Commander Elara Voss ran her routine diagnostics, the ship’s hum a familiar lullaby. Then, a vibration. Not the *Voyager*’s thrusters, but something deeper, resonating through the deck plates into her bones. She paused, ear pressed against the cold metal. *Thrum… thrum…*

She looked up. The stars weren’t just *there*; they were *listening*. Not with light, but with a palpable, *pressure* in the void. A low, subsonic thrumming, felt more than heard, vibrating the oxygen in her lungs, making her teeth ache. The crimson dust clinging to the viewport seemed to pulse in time.

*Thrum… thrum…*

She scrambled to the main console, fingers flying over keys. Spectral analysis. The stars weren’t burning gas, weren’t distant suns. They were *ears*. Vast, ancient, resonant membranes stretched across the fabric of space, tuned to the faintest tremors of existence. And *she* was the tremor. The *Voyager*’s thrum, her heartbeat, the frantic hammering of her own blood – it wasn’t just *heard* by the stars; it was *sought*.

A single, crystal-clear note, like a tuning fork struck in the absolute dark, echoed in her helmet. Not sound, but a *presence*, cold and vast, focusing *on her*. The stars weren’t lights. They were the universe’s ears, and she had just made a sound they’d been waiting decades to hear. The silence that followed wasn’t empty; it was the sound of an infinite, indifferent listener leaning in. The cold wasn’t from space anymore. It was the chill of being *known* by something that had never known a soul. She couldn’t move. She couldn’t breathe. The stars were no longer the destination. They were the audience. And she was the performance. (298 words)
