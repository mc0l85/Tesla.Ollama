**The Light That Wasn't There**

The first time Captain Mara Voss saw the anomaly, it was a whisper against the vacuum of her helmet. A faint, metallic taste lingered on her tongue as she floated outside the observation dome, the stars glittering like shattered glass. She had spent a year charting the edge of the Helix Nebula, mapping gravitational ripples that no one else could detect. The data streamed in crisp, white numbers—until the night sky began to *sing*.

At first it was just a hum, a low vibration that resonated through the metal of her suit. Then the constellations flickered, not with the steady pulse of distant suns, but with an irregular, almost deliberate pulse. The colors shifted from the familiar white and amber to sickly greens and bruised purples, as if the heavens were bruised. Mara’s breath hitched; the air tasted metallic, like iron filings, and a cold that seeped into her bones made her skin prickle.

She reached out with a gloved hand, and the stars seemed to recoil, pulling back as though they were aware of her touch. The pattern they formed was no longer random; it spelled a word in a language she could not read, yet understood in a primal way: *WATCH*. The realization hit her like a wave of static—these were not lights at all, but eyes, ancient and indifferent, watching her from the void.

Mara’s heart hammered against her ribs, each beat echoing in the hollow of her chest. She tried to call back to the ship, but the comms crackled with static, as if the very air resisted her voice. The stars tightened their grip, and the universe seemed to close in, the edges of her vision blurring into a blackness that felt like the inside of a coffin.

When she finally managed to pull herself back into the safety of the dome, the stars outside were gone. In their place, a single, unblinking point of darkness stared at her—an abyss that promised no answers, only the unsettling certainty that some lights were never meant to be seen. The taste of iron lingered, and Mara knew she would never look at the night sky again without feeling the weight of that unseen gaze.
