- CRISPR-Cas9, discovered as a bacterial immune system and reprogrammed by Doudna and Charpentier in 2012, enables precise DNA editing and earned the 2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.  
- The technology has transformative applications in agriculture (disease-resistant crops), medicine (e.g., 2023 FDA-approved sickle cell therapy), and basic research (gene function studies).  
- Ethical concerns over germline editing, highlighted by the 2018 He Jiankui controversy, coexist with significant commercial growth, evidenced by over $5 billion in CRISPR startup investments in 2023.
